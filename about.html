About Me

I am an Assistant Professor in the Department of Computer Science at the University of San Francisco. I am also the Graduate Director for the Masters in Science in Computer Science (MSCS) program, and teach courses for the Masters in Science in Analytics (MSAN) program.

I graduated with my Ph.D. in Computer Science from the University of California, Davis under the guidance of Matt Bishop, Sean Peisert, and many others at the SecLab. I received my B.S. in Computer Science from the University of Nebraska at Omaha, studying networking and computer security with Ken Dick and Blaine Burnham at the Peter Kiewit Institute. I also frequently collaborate with my significant other, Sean Whalen.

I have many research interests, including data visualization, computer security, and computer science education. Please see my curriculum vitae for more information.

Background

I grew up in Omaha, Nebraska. While I love the west coast, I still miss the green rolling hills of corn, gorgeous skies and sunsets, dramatic storms, open spaces, amazing zoo, and four seasons. I moved to Davis, California (pictured above) in 2002 to work on my graduate degree. I loved the Davis Farmer's Market, walking down Olive Tree Lane, and exploring the Village Homes neighborhood (where the street names come from Tolkien books).

In 2010, I moved to San Francisco near Ocean Beach (pictured above) and love it. I enjoy walking along the dunes—even on the foggy gray days. I love the laid-back culture of the neighborhood, and the coffee in the area is amazing.

Family

I grew up in a family of five with two younger sisters, and an extended family in South Korea. While we are geographically spread out, we are all still very close.

I got my passion for computers and teaching from my dad (picture above). I used to tag along to his work on weekends and watch him work with huge mainframes. My own first computer was a hand-me down from his work and ran OS2. I eventually started to help him at work, which led to my first internship at PKS Information Systems in 1997.

Sadly, my dad passed away in 2006 right after his 50th birthday to pancreatic cancer. He was a great father and mentor, and respected by many. I miss him every day. Pancreatic cancer has only a 6% five year survival rate. If you want to help change that, I suggest you look into the Pancreatic Cancer Action Network at http://www.pancan.org/ online.

I met my significant other (and frequent collaborator) Sean Whalen at the University of Nebraska at Omaha. See node99.org for more information on his research interests.
